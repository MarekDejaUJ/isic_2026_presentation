The Generalization Impasse in LIS

Library and Information Science (LIS) boasts rich theoretical frameworks. However, empirical studies are often bound and limited by statistical and causal methods that default to broad, population-wide generalization.

A typical information behavior (IB) in context research project requires its own specific design and methodological development. The full potential of in-context research is not in chasing sweeping averages, but in supporting decision-making based on local evidence.

Rather than relying solely on statistical inference for generalization, we need methods designed to evaluate local evidence and context-specific configurations.

Subjective Structures & Effects of Causes

"Subjectivity is the very area where the effect of cases is located."

The Fuzzy Subjective Structure Model (FSSM) captures how the effects of a cause—whether adjacent (proximal interaction) or coercive (structural policies/norms)—reside within the subjective structure of human perception rather than acting as deterministic drivers.

Environmental and institutional factors do not directly force behavior; instead, their impact is shaped by how individuals subjectively perceive and experience these affordances and constraints.

Beyond the "Perfect Average"

Traditional regression models rely on mean estimates that represent a vague abstraction of a "perfect human being"—which none of us is. Such approaches discard individual autonomy of thoughts.

To respect this autonomy, we need subjectivity mapping that assesses:

  • Typicality: Regularities that hold for nearly all cases in a group.
  • Necessity: Non-compensatory system bottlenecks (critical gatekeepers).
  • Probability: The likelihood of an individual reacting or behaving as an effect of context causes.

FSSM maps these dynamics by looking at how far and how close individuals are toward a modeled desirable threshold, rather than calculating aggregate averages that mask individual realities.

The Arrow-Reversal & Context-First Skeleton

Information behavior theory dictates: Context → Perception → Behavior. Yet, regression models often violate this, treating perceptions as drivers of other perceptions or reversing the causal order for statistical convenience.

FSSM enforces the theoretical skeleton: environmental factors (Information Culture and Management) act as upstream enabling context conditions, which shape downstream behavior through intermediate subjective perceptions.

The Four Causal Claims of FSSM

1. Typical Sufficiency (α)

"If X is high, Y is typically high." Evaluates whether organizational policies usually suffice to produce the behavior, allowing rare exceptions.

2. Typical Necessity (ε)

"If Y is high, X is rarely low." Identifies non-compensatory bottlenecks—conditions without which high outcomes are impossible.

3. Probabilistic Sufficiency (Δ / Uplift)

"Does raising X increase the probability of high Y?" Measures the probability-scale contrast after adjusting for covariates.

4. Probabilistic Necessity (β)

"If X is low, high Y is unlikely." Converts the bottleneck constraint into a concrete, counterfactual risk statement.

Dual Logic: Latent vs. Composite Route

FSSM offers a flexible, dual-route design to address different analytical goals and data realities:

  • Route A (Latent Variable Route - PLS-SEM + NCA): Extends latent structural models. Binds structural path estimation with necessity bottleneck diagnostics to achieve both population generalization and subjective structure exploration.
  • Route B (Raw Composite Route - NCA + Case Weighting): Bypasses latent modeling demands using raw composites. Enables a case-level "finding people" approach, allowing researchers to study teams, even small teams, precisely to diagnose their local information needs and behaviors.

Fuzzy Concept & Subjectivity Mapping

Continuous latent and composite scores are mapped onto a fuzzy membership interval [0, 1] relative to empirical thresholds and bottlenecks:

  • μS(x): The degree to which an individual case meets the necessary context bottleneck.
  • μY(y): The degree of outcome achievement.

This fuzzy mapping prevents cases near arbitrary cutoffs from distorting the results. It honors individual autonomy by showing exactly how close or how far each unique case is from the necessary thresholds, enabling true person-in-context interpretation.
