Navigating subjectivity in causal context

A non-deterministic approach for information behaviour research

The generalization impasse in LIS

  • Direction: fit statistics cannot decide whether information behaviour causes culture, or culture enables behaviour.
  • Logic: average effects do not show whether a context condition is a non-compensatory bottleneck.
  • Aggregation: population estimates can hide the specific person, team, library, or institutional configuration that matters.

FSSM evaluates context-specific configurations by separating typical and probabilistic forms of sufficiency and necessity.

Subjective structures & effects of causes

"Subjectivity is the very area where the effect of causes is located."

The Fuzzy Subjective Structure Model captures how the effects of a cause, whether adjacent (proximal interaction) or coercive (structural policies, culture, and norms), reside within the subjective structure of human perception rather than acting as deterministic drivers.

It evaluates subjective structure by applying rules about effects of causes: sufficiency, necessity, adjusted probability contrasts, and case membership.

Environmental and institutional factors do not directly force behaviour; their impact is shaped by how individuals subjectively perceive and experience affordances and constraints.

Beyond the "perfect average"

Regression models produce parameter estimates: central tendencies across a population that abstract away the individual variation information behaviour research most needs to understand.

FSSM shifts focus to subjectivity mapping, assessing:

  • Typical sufficiency: the outcome usually follows when $X$ clears $T_X$.
  • Typical necessity: high outcomes rarely occur below the required level.
  • Probabilistic sufficiency: adjusted uplift when $X$ clears $T_X$.
  • Probabilistic necessity: high-outcome probability when $X$ is insufficient.

FSSM maps how far or close each person is from an empirically grounded threshold, rather than computing aggregate summaries that mask individual positions.

Arrow-reversal & context-first structure

Information behaviour theory dictates: Context → Perception → Behaviour. Yet models can still place behaviour upstream and treat a symmetric association as if it established causal direction.

A common modelling choice puts information sharing on the left: individual and group sharing predict a richer, more open information-sharing culture. Associatively this may hold. In causal terms, however, individual information behaviour does not cause the group's information-sharing culture; it helps explain the individual's acceptance of that culture. Group behaviour, norms, and expectations can shape individual behaviour through acculturation and coercion. FSSM requires the context-first structure: Management and Culture upstream; Self-Efficacy and Empowerment intermediate; Information Behaviour downstream.

The four causal claims of FSSM

1. Typical Sufficiency (α)

"If $X \geq T_X$, then $Y \geq T_Y$ for most cases." Evaluates whether context conditions usually suffice to produce the behaviour, allowing rare exceptions.

2. Typical Necessity (ϵ)

ϵ is the exception rate among high-outcome cases. Low ϵ supports a non-compensatory bottleneck claim.

3. Probabilistic Sufficiency (Δ / Uplift)

"Does crossing $T_X$ increase the probability of high $Y$?" Probability-scale contrast on a weighted pseudo-population under adjustment assumptions.

4. Probabilistic Necessity (β)

"If $X$ is below its bottleneck, high $Y$ is unlikely." Estimates high-outcome probability when the required level is not met.

Four causal claims: mathematical form

Let $b(y_i)=(T_X/T_Y)y_i$ be the case-specific bottleneck implied by the NCA ceiling. $T_Y$ is the study's outcome target. $T_X$ is the NCA-derived bottleneck for that target. $\Pr^*$ denotes probability on the weighted pseudo-population and requires explicit identification assumptions.

Typical sufficiency (α)

$$\alpha = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i \,\mathbf{1}(x_i \geq T_X)\,\mathbf{1}(y_i \geq T_Y)}{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i \,\mathbf{1}(x_i \geq T_X)}$$

Typical necessity exception rate (ϵ)

$$\varepsilon = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i \,\mathbf{1}(y_i \geq T_Y)\,\mathbf{1}(x_i < b(y_i))}{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i \,\mathbf{1}(y_i \geq T_Y)}$$

Probabilistic sufficiency (Δ)

$$\Delta = \Pr^*(Y \!\geq\! T_Y \mid X \!\geq\! T_X) - \Pr^*(Y \!\geq\! T_Y \mid X \!<\! T_X)$$

Probabilistic necessity (β)

$$\beta = \Pr^*(Y \geq T_Y \mid X < T_X)$$

Dual logic: latent vs. composite route

FSSM offers a flexible, dual-route design to address different analytical goals and data realities:

  • Route A (Latent Variable Route, PLS-SEM + NCA): Extends latent structural models. It supports population-level modelling through structural paths while attaching necessity bottlenecks and case-level diagnostics under explicit assumptions.
  • Route B (Raw Composite Route, NCA + Case Weighting): Bypasses latent modelling using raw composites. NCA identifies the threshold $T_X$ for a specific desired $T_Y$, and every respondent is located on the resulting fuzzy membership scale. Enables a case-level "finding people" approach, allowing researchers to study teams, even small ones, to diagnose local information needs and behaviours precisely.

Double subjectivity: entropy weighting & IPW

FSSM addresses subjectivity at two distinct levels, each requiring its own correction:

1. Measurement subjectivity: respondents interpret survey items through disciplinary and role-based lenses. Entropy weighting up-weights indicators with more discriminating information when forming construct scores:

$$E_j=-\frac{1}{\log K}\sum_k p_{jk}\log p_{jk}, \qquad d_j=1-E_j, \qquad w_j^{\mathrm{EW}}=\frac{d_j}{\sum_l d_l}$$

2. Confounding: observed group differences may reflect discipline, seniority, or institutional role rather than the construct of interest. Inverse Probability Weighting re-balances the sample to approximate a pseudo-randomized condition:

$$w_i^{\mathrm{IPW}} = \frac{D_i}{\hat{e}(c_i)} + \frac{1-D_i}{1-\hat{e}(c_i)}, \qquad \hat{e}(c_i) = \Pr(D_i = 1 \mid c_i)$$

Together, these layers support adjusted threshold contrasts under measured-confounding, overlap, and model-specification assumptions.

Fuzzy concept & subjectivity mapping

Construct scores are rescaled to 0-100 and mapped onto fuzzy membership intervals relative to empirical thresholds and bottlenecks:

  • $\mu_N(x,y)$: degree to which an individual case meets the case-specific necessary context bottleneck.
  • $\mu_S(x)$: degree to which the condition exceeds the target bottleneck for sufficiency contrasts.
  • $\mu_Y(y)$: the degree of outcome achievement relative to the desired target $T_Y$.

This fuzzy mapping preserves case heterogeneity by showing how close or far each case is from the necessary threshold, enabling person-in-context interpretation without binary categorization.

Fuzzy membership: mathematical form

On 0-100 construct scores, with $b(y_i)=(T_X/T_Y)y_i$:

$$\mu_S(x_i)=\max\!\left(0,\min\!\left(1,\frac{x_i-T_X}{100-T_X}\right)\right)$$ $$\mu_N(x_i,y_i)=\min\!\left(1,\frac{x_i}{b(y_i)}\right)$$

Degree of outcome achievement for $Y$:

$$\mu_Y(y_i) = \min\!\left(1,\; \frac{y_i}{T_Y}\right)$$

The pair $\bigl(\mu_N(x_i,y_i),\,\mu_Y(y_i)\bigr)$ locates every respondent on a context-threshold plane. The institutional environment is an ongoing context that continuously shapes each individual's subjective structure. Threshold-seeking replaces point-treatment logic: the question is not "did an intervention occur?" but "how far is this person from the enabling threshold?"

Thank you

FSSM moves information behaviour research beyond the abstraction of the average human toward actionable, localized evidence. It is a procedure for evaluating causal claims, not for identifying causation from cross-sectional data by algorithmic fiat. Causal interpretation remains contingent on the identification assumptions the analyst supplies in the design.
