The Fuzzy Subjective Structure Model

Evaluating Causal Claims for Non-Deterministic Inference on Subjective Constructs

Marek Deja

The Generalization Impasse in LIS

Library and Information Science has built rich theoretical frameworks. Yet empirical studies are often bound by statistical and causal methods that default to broad, population-wide generalization.

A typical information behaviour research project in context requires its own specific design and methodological development. The full potential of in-context research is not in chasing sweeping averages, but in supporting decision-making based on local evidence.

Rather than relying solely on statistical inference for generalization, we need methods designed to evaluate local evidence and context-specific configurations.

Subjective Structures & Effects of Causes

"Subjectivity is the very area where the effect of causes is located."

The Fuzzy Subjective Structure Model captures how the effects of a cause—whether adjacent (proximal interaction) or coercive (structural policies, culture, and norms)—reside within the subjective structure of human perception rather than acting as deterministic drivers.

Environmental and institutional factors do not directly force behaviour; their impact is shaped by how individuals subjectively perceive and experience affordances and constraints.

Beyond the "Perfect Average"

Traditional regression models rely on mean estimates that represent a vague abstraction of a "perfect human being"—which none of us is. Such approaches discard individual autonomy of thoughts.

To respect this autonomy, we need subjectivity mapping that assesses:

  • Typicality: regularities that hold for nearly all cases in a group.
  • Necessity: non-compensatory system bottlenecks—critical gatekeepers.
  • Probability: the likelihood of an individual reacting or behaving as an effect of context causes.

FSSM maps these dynamics by asking how far and how close individuals are toward a modeled desirable threshold, rather than calculating aggregate averages that mask individual realities.

The Arrow-Reversal & Context-First Skeleton

Information behaviour theory dictates: Context → Perception → Behaviour. Yet regression models often violate this, treating perceptions as drivers of other perceptions or reversing the causal order for statistical convenience.

Many empirical models place individual behaviour on the left-hand side and treat culture or management as downstream correlates. This inverts the causal story: compliance with open data policies is not an "effect" that produces culture—it is the institution's norms, governance clarity, and trust that enable action. FSSM enforces the context-first skeleton: Management and Culture as upstream enabling conditions; Self-Efficacy and Empowerment as intermediate perceptions; Collaboration as the downstream behaviour.

The Four Causal Claims of FSSM

1. Typical Sufficiency (α)

"If $X \geq t_X$, then $Y \geq t_Y$ for most cases." Evaluates whether context conditions usually suffice to produce the behaviour, allowing rare exceptions.

2. Typical Necessity (ϵ)

"If $Y$ is high, $X$ is rarely below its bottleneck." Identifies non-compensatory gatekeepers—conditions without which high outcomes are uncommon.

3. Probabilistic Sufficiency (Δ / Uplift)

"Does raising $X$ above $t_X$ increase the probability of high $Y$?" Probability-scale contrast on an IPW-balanced pseudo-population.

4. Probabilistic Necessity (β)

"If $X$ is below its bottleneck, high $Y$ is unlikely." Converts the constraint into a concrete, counterfactual risk statement.

Four Causal Claims: Mathematical Form

All four claims are anchored to the same empirical thresholds: $t_X$ (NCA-derived bottleneck) and $t_Y$ (desired outcome level). $\Pr^*$ denotes probability on the IPW-balanced pseudo-population; $w_i$ are normalized case weights.

Typical Sufficiency (α)

$$\alpha = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i \,\mathbf{1}(x_i \geq t_X)\,\mathbf{1}(y_i \geq t_Y)}{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i \,\mathbf{1}(x_i \geq t_X)} \;\geq\; 0.85$$

Typical Necessity (ϵ)

$$\varepsilon = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i \,\mathbf{1}(y_i \geq t_Y)\,\mathbf{1}(x_i < t_X)}{\displaystyle\sum_i w_i \,\mathbf{1}(y_i \geq t_Y)} \;\leq\; 0.15$$

Probabilistic Sufficiency (Δ)

$$\Delta = \Pr^*(Y \!\geq\! t_Y \mid X \!\geq\! t_X) - \Pr^*(Y \!\geq\! t_Y \mid X \!<\! t_X)$$

Probabilistic Necessity (β)

$$\beta = \Pr^*(Y \geq t_Y \mid X < t_X) \;\leq\; 0.20$$

Dual Logic: Latent vs. Composite Route

FSSM offers a flexible, dual-route design to address different analytical goals and data realities:

  • Route A (Latent Variable Route — PLS-SEM + NCA): Extends latent structural models. Binds structural path estimation with necessity bottleneck diagnostics to achieve both population generalization and case-wise subjective structure exploration. The latent variable route provides a sound ground for causal generalization with reversal to case-level causal detection.
  • Route B (Raw Composite Route — NCA + Case Weighting): Bypasses latent modelling using raw composites. NCA identifies the threshold $t_X$ for a specific desired $t_Y$, and every respondent is located on the resulting fuzzy membership scale. Enables a case-level "finding people" approach—allowing researchers to study teams, even small ones, to diagnose local information needs and behaviours precisely.

Double Subjectivity: Entropy Weighting & IPW

FSSM addresses subjectivity at two distinct levels, each requiring its own correction:

1. Measurement subjectivity — respondents interpret survey items through disciplinary and role-based lenses. Entropy weighting up-weights indicators with discriminating, informative response patterns and down-weights noisy ones:

$$w_j^{\mathrm{EW}} \;\propto\; H(j) = -\sum_k p_{jk}\log p_{jk}$$

2. Confounding — observed group differences may reflect discipline, seniority, or institutional role rather than the construct of interest. Inverse Probability Weighting re-balances the sample to approximate a pseudo-randomized condition:

$$w_i^{\mathrm{IPW}} = \frac{D_i}{\hat{e}(c_i)} + \frac{1-D_i}{1-\hat{e}(c_i)}, \qquad \hat{e}(c_i) = \Pr(D_i = 1 \mid c_i)$$

Together, these two weighting layers let FSSM move from association to a principled approximation of intervention contrasts under explicitly stated identification assumptions.

Fuzzy Concept & Subjectivity Mapping

Continuous latent and composite scores are mapped onto a fuzzy membership interval $[0, 1]$ relative to empirical thresholds and bottlenecks:

  • $\mu_S(x)$: the degree to which an individual case meets the necessary context bottleneck—how close a case is to crossing the enabling threshold.
  • $\mu_Y(y)$: the degree of outcome achievement relative to the desired target $t_Y$.

This fuzzy mapping prevents cases near arbitrary cutoffs from distorting the results. It honours individual autonomy by showing exactly how close or far each case is from the necessary threshold—enabling true person-in-context interpretation without binary categorization.

Fuzzy Membership: Mathematical Form

Membership in the necessary bottleneck for condition $X$—how close a case is to the enabling threshold $t_X$:

$$\mu_S(x_i) = \begin{cases} 0 & x_i = 0 \\[6pt] \dfrac{x_i}{t_X} & 0 < x_i < t_X \\[10pt] 1 & x_i \geq t_X \end{cases}$$

Degree of outcome achievement for $Y$:

$$\mu_Y(y_i) = \min\!\left(1,\; \frac{y_i}{t_Y}\right)$$

The pair $\bigl(\mu_S(x_i),\,\mu_Y(y_i)\bigr)$ locates every respondent on what we might call a self-treatment scale. Rather than a discrete, time-bounded intervention, the institutional environment is an ongoing exogenous context that continuously shapes each individual's endogenous subjective structure. Threshold-seeking replaces point-treatment logic: the question is not "did an intervention occur?" but "how far is this person from the enabling threshold?"—closer to Li and Pearl's Probability of Necessity and Probability of Sufficiency in spirit, but expressed as a graded, person-in-context reading over an ongoing environmental condition.

Thank You

FSSM moves information behaviour research beyond the abstraction of the average human toward actionable, localized evidence. It is a procedure for evaluating causal claims—not for identifying causation from cross-sectional data by algorithmic fiat. Causal interpretation remains contingent on the identification assumptions the analyst supplies in the design.
